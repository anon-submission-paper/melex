MelEx: A new dataset, algorithm, and comparative evaluation for melody extraction in symbolic music


[Paper] | [Code]

        Melody is often considered an essential part of music, and melody extraction in symbolic music (MESM) is one of the earliest topics in the field of music information retrieval (MIR). With the development of machine learning and deep learning, most recent MESM approaches are learning-based as opposed to rules-based, raising questions: i)~how differing approaches to MESM compare, and ii)~the robustness of existing solutions. To investigate these issues, we introduce a new dataset that contains 474 measures of 40 fully orchestrated music excerpts in MIDI format, with two professionally annotated melodies per excerpt. We also propose a new rule-based algorithm for MESM named MelodyExtractor (MelEx). The results of a comparative evaluation in the form a listening study show that MelEx outperforms two learning-based approaches (MIDI-BERT and LStoM) for "melody correctness" on our dataset. In terms of robustness, results indicate a significant drop-off in performance of learning-based approaches MIDI-BERT and LStoM when applied to music beyond their training dataset. While the drop-off is not surprising, contributions of this paper include: quantifying the drop-off; underlining the need for re-training existing ML approaches on a greater diversity of data; and proposing a rules-based approach that can be utilized in future comparative evaluations.

Rating distributions by datasets

Violin plots for rating (1–7) distributions of 11 dimensions introduced in Section 5.2.3 on the whole dataset. The envelope represents the distribution of responses; the lines indicate rating scales; the horizontal line goes from the lower quartile through the median to the upper quartile. We observed that there is a significant drop-off in ratings for melody correctness of learning-based approaches (MIDI-BERT and LStoM) when applied to music beyond their training dataset. We also observed that an approach that receives higher ratings for melody correctness tends to receive higher ratings for perception characters, as well as is human, and willingness to use such a melody extraction system.

Rating distributions by annotators

Violin plots for rating (1–7) distributions of 11 dimensions introduced in Section 5.2.3 on the whole dataset, where "a1 - a8" represent melodies annotated by 8 professional annotators separately. The envelope represents the distribution of responses; the lines indicate rating scales; the horizontal line goes from the lower quartile through the median to the upper quartile. We observed that there is a relatively high level of objectivity in terms of how listeners rate the melody correctness of human-annotated melody extraction, and the distribution of melody correctness ratings is broadly consistent across eight annotators, but melodies annotated by annotator "a7" receive a lower rating for "easy to remember" (remember) than that from other annotators.
